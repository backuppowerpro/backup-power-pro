What goes wrong.

A customer buys a generator labeled "30 amp" on the box. They get it home, slide it out of the carton, and look at the back panel. There's a round outlet, three prongs in a curved arc, that looks right. So they assume it's the standard hookup we asked them to provide and schedule the install.

When we show up, we find one of two things. Either the outlet is a TT-30Rthe round 3-prong used on RV pedestals, or it's an L5-30Rthe twist-lock 3-prong used in light commercial work. Both are rated 30 amps. Both are 120 volts only. Neither one can carry the 240V circuits the transfer switch needs to feed your panel.

The fix at that point is not cheap. The generator goes back, restocking fee included, and we re-spec to a 4-prong unit, usually a L14-30R or 14-50R. That's a wasted afternoon and roughly $400 of generator the customer doesn't get back. The whole thing is avoidable with a 4-second prong count before purchase.

Same "30 amp" label. Different worlds.

The label "30 amp" on a generator can mean either 240V (compatible) or 120V (not compatible). Always check prong count.

How to spot it in 5 seconds.

Count the prongs. Read the code stamped on the face of the outlet. Match it to this table.
