## SECTION 1: PORTABLE GENERATOR MODELS — OUTLET CONFIGURATIONS

### HOW TO READ THIS SECTION
- **L14-30R** = NEMA L14-30, 120/240V, 30A, 4-prong twist-lock. THE standard outlet for residential transfer switch / interlock connections. This is the outlet BPP connects to with a 4-wire 30A cord.
- **L14-20R** = NEMA L14-20, 120/240V, 20A, 4-prong twist-lock. Less common. Works for lower-wattage connections but limits to 20A (4,800W at 240V).
- **14-50R** = NEMA 14-50, 120/240V, 50A, straight-blade (same receptacle as an EV charger or RV 50A). Found on large generators. Can be used with a 50A inlet installation instead of 30A.
- **L5-30R** = NEMA L5-30, 120V only, 30A, 3-prong twist-lock. RV receptacle. **Cannot be used for a home interlock connection** — it's 120V only.
- **TT-30R** = NEMA TT-30, 120V only, 30A, recreational vehicle outlet. **Cannot be used for a home interlock connection.**
- **5-20R** = Standard 120V 20A household outlet. Not usable for whole-home connection.

**KEY GOTCHA FOR ALEX:** Always confirm the generator has a 4-wire 240V outlet (L14-30R, 14-50R, or L14-20R). Generators with only 120V outputs (L5-30R, TT-30R, 5-20R) CANNOT support a standard interlock/transfer switch installation and would require a 120V-only subpanel approach, which BPP does not typically offer. If a customer's generator only has 120V outlets, flag this immediately.

## SECTION 2: ELECTRICAL PANEL BRANDS — VISUAL IDENTIFICATION

### IMPORTANT PRINCIPLE
Panel brand matters because interlock kit compatibility is panel-specific. The wrong interlock kit will not fit or will not function safely. Key identifies the panel brand BEFORE ordering materials. Alex should help customers describe what they see so Key can confirm the panel type.

---

### 2A. SQUARE D

**Two product lines — QO and Homeline — are NOT interchangeable.**

**Square D QO (Premium Residential / Light Commercial)**
- Breaker appearance: Compact, 3/4" wide per pole. Black handles. Feature a prominent RED INDICATOR FLAG that pops out when a breaker trips (the defining visual feature of QO). Handle snaps to a mid-position between ON and OFF when tripped.
- Bus bar: V-shaped copper bus — highest quality.
- Panel door: Gray metal door, "Square D" name and logo prominent. Interior label typically says "QO" series. The panel door is sometimes beige/tan on older units.
- Spacing: Tight, compact. QO breakers are physically smaller than Homeline.
- Interlock compatibility: EXCELLENT. Square D manufactures their own interlock kit for QO panels. Third-party kits (Interlock Technologies, GenInterlock) also available. QO is probably the best-supported panel for interlock kit availability.
- Notes: Found in higher-end new construction and commercial. Often installed by professional electricians. Can accommodate 3-pole breakers.

**Square D Homeline (Economy Residential)**
- Breaker appearance: 1" wide per pole. Black handles. NO red trip indicator flag. When tripped, handle moves to the OFF position (or a center position depending on model — less visually obvious than QO).
- Bus bar: Flat aluminum bus.
- Panel door: Gray metal, "Square D Homeline" label. Often has a plastic breaker directory card holder on the inside of the door.
- Spacing: 1" per pole, slightly larger than QO.
- Interlock compatibility: GOOD. Square D Homeline has dedicated interlock kits. Third-party kits also available. Must verify panel amperage (100A, 150A, 200A) and main breaker position (center vs. offset).
- Notes: The most common Square D panel in builder-grade residential construction. If a customer says "Square D" without specifying, ask if the breakers have a little red indicator — if yes, it's QO; if no, it's likely Homeline.

**KEY DISTINCTION FOR ALEX:** QO and Homeline breakers are NOT interchangeable and use different interlock kits. Always identify which line.

### 2B. SIEMENS / MURRAY

**Siemens and Murray panels are functionally identical — same bus design, same breaker type.**
Murray was a standalone brand that Siemens acquired. Murray-branded panels use Siemens-type QP breakers. An interlock kit for Siemens fits Murray and vice versa.

**Siemens (and Murray)**
- Breaker appearance: 1" wide per pole. BLACK handles. No color-coded indicator. Handle moves to center when tripped.
- Breaker designation: "QP" series (standard single/double pole), "QPP" (tandem/piggyback). Murray uses "MP-T" and "MPP-T" designations for the same physical breakers.
- Bus bar: Typically 5/8" to 1" spacing. Bolt-on or plug-in depending on generation.
- Panel door: Gray painted metal. Siemens panels often have a "Siemens" logo embossed or labeled prominently. Murray panels say "Murray" but look visually similar.
- Interlock compatibility: EXCELLENT. Siemens/Murray is one of the most widely supported brands. GenInterlock, Interlock Technologies all make Siemens/Murray-specific kits. Must identify 100A vs. 150A vs. 200A panel.
- Notes: One of the most common panels in SC residential homes built 1990s–present. Siemens and Murray panels are extremely common in the Carolinas.

### 2C. EATON (BR Line and CH Line)

**Eaton has two distinct residential product lines. They use DIFFERENT interlock kits.**

**Eaton BR (Budget Residential)**
- Breaker appearance: 1" wide per pole. BLACK handles. Standard toggle-style trip mechanism — handle moves to OFF when tripped. No trip-to-center feature.
- Bus connections: Aluminum connections (economy design).
- Panel door: Gray metal. Labeled "Eaton" or sometimes still labeled "Cutler-Hammer" (same company). May say "BR" on the interior.
- Interlock compatibility: GOOD. Dedicated BR-series interlock kits exist. Must confirm panel amperage and main breaker location.
- Notes: Economy residential line. Most common Eaton panel in builder-grade construction.

**Eaton CH (Commercial / Premium Residential)**
- Breaker appearance: 3/4" wide per pole. DISTINCTIVE TAN/SANDALWOOD colored handles — this is the clearest visual identifier. Has a "trip-to-center" mechanism where the handle moves to a center position between ON and OFF when tripped.
- Bus connections: Copper bus connections for higher reliability.
- Panel door: Gray metal. May say "Cutler-Hammer" or "Eaton CH." Interior label confirms CH series.
- Interlock compatibility: GOOD. CH-series interlock kits differ from BR kits — cannot mix them up. Dedicated CH kits available from Interlock Technologies and others.
- Notes: Premium residential / light commercial. The tan handle is the unmistakable visual tell.

**EATON / CUTLER-HAMMER RELATIONSHIP:**
- Cutler-Hammer is NOT a separate brand — it is an older name for Eaton. Eaton acquired Cutler-Hammer in 1978.
- "Cutler-Hammer CH" and "Eaton CH" are the same thing. "Cutler-Hammer BR" and "Eaton BR" are the same thing.
- Many older panels still say "Cutler-Hammer" on the door — treat them as Eaton for interlock kit purposes.
- Part numbers are identical; the brand name change is cosmetic.

**KEY DISTINCTION FOR ALEX:** BR = black handles, CH = tan/sandalwood handles. They require different interlock kits.

---

### 2D. GE / GENERAL ELECTRIC

**GE panels are common in homes built through the 1990s and early 2000s, less common in new construction today (GE's electrical division was sold to ABB).**

- Breaker appearance: 1" wide per pole. BLACK or DARK GRAY handles. GE is notable for inconsistency in breaker appearance across generations — visual identification by handle color alone is unreliable. Main breaker designation: "THQL" (standard plug-in) or "THQB" (bolt-in). Breakers labeled "GE Q-Line" or just "GE" on the face.
- Bus bar: Standard residential design.
- Panel door: Gray metal. Prominent "GE" logo or "General Electric" lettering. Often has a distinctive oval or rectangular GE emblem on the cover.
- Interlock compatibility: GOOD. GE panels have dedicated interlock kits. Verify 100A vs. 150A vs. 200A and panel model.
- Notes: GE sold its industrial/residential electrical business to ABB in 2018. New panels may be labeled "ABB" or "GE by ABB." Older panels in the field are common and parts/kits remain available.

---

### 2E. LEVITON

- Leviton entered the load center market relatively recently (circa 2010s).
- Breaker appearance: Modern, clean appearance. Breakers follow current NEC spacing standards. Black handles.
- Panel door: Clean modern aesthetic. "Leviton" branding.
- Interlock compatibility: GOOD — Leviton follows current NEC spacing standards, making modern engineered interlocks compatible.
- Notes: Less common in Upstate SC residential market — Leviton is newer to panels. If encountered, treat like a modern Square D Homeline in terms of interlock approach.

---

### 2F. ZINSCO — HAZARDOUS PANEL

**VISUAL IDENTIFICATION:**
- Breaker appearance: DISTINCTIVE MULTI-COLORED BREAKER HANDLES — red, green, and blue toggles (color-coded by circuit). This is the #1 visual tell for Zinsco.
- The panel may be labeled "Zinsco," "Sylvania," "Magnetrip," or "GTE-Sylvania" (Zinsco was acquired by GTE-Sylvania in 1973).
- Bus bar: Aluminum bus that is prone to corrosion.
- Panel age: Installed 1963–1981.

**WHY IT'S DANGEROUS:**
- Breakers can weld themselves to the aluminum bus bar. Even when switched to OFF, the circuit may remain energized.
- Breakers fail to trip under overload — arc fault and fire risk.
- The aluminum bus corrodes over time, creating high-resistance connections that overheat.
- Insurance companies in SC frequently refuse to write policies or will charge significantly higher premiums for homes with Zinsco panels.
- Estimated to contribute to thousands of residential fires annually (combined with FPE).

**BPP ACTION:** Do NOT install an interlock kit on a Zinsco panel. Flag it to Key immediately. The customer needs a full panel replacement before BPP can proceed. This is not a safety shortcut — it is an actual hazard.

---

### 2G. FEDERAL PACIFIC ELECTRIC (FPE) — STAB-LOK — HAZARDOUS PANEL

**VISUAL IDENTIFICATION:**
- Breaker appearance: Breakers have a distinctive THIN RED STRIPE running horizontally across the front face of each breaker. This is the clearest visual tell.
- The panel door or interior label will say "Federal Pacific Electric," "FPE," or "Stab-Lok." Look for the "Stab-Lok" name near the center or side of the panel interior.
- Breaker handles: Typically dark gray or black, with the red accent stripe.
- Panel age: Installed 1950s–1990s. Extremely common in homes built in the 1960s–1980s.

**WHY IT'S DANGEROUS:**
- Stab-Lok breakers have documented failure rates as high as 60% in some studies — meaning they may fail to trip during an overload or fault.
- The bus bar connection ("stab-lok" design) is prone to loosening, creating arcing and heat.
- Estimated 2,800 residential fires and $40 million in property damage per year attributable to FPE panels.
- Many insurance companies in SC will not insure a home with an FPE panel, or will require replacement before binding coverage.

**BPP ACTION:** Do NOT install an interlock kit on an FPE/Stab-Lok panel. Flag to Key immediately. Customer needs full panel replacement. There is no safe workaround.

---

### 2H. CHALLENGER

**VISUAL IDENTIFICATION:**
- Breaker appearance: BLACK breaker handles. Look for the "Challenger" name printed or embossed on the breakers themselves.
- Panel door: The "Challenger" name appears on the latch/handle of the outside panel door. Interior label also confirms brand. Door latch label is the fastest way to identify in the field.
- History: "Challenger" was introduced in 1973 by GTE-Sylvania (same parent as Zinsco). Changed ownership four times before being discontinued. Final owner was Eaton Corporation. Installed in hundreds of thousands of homes 1980s–1990s.

**SAFETY STATUS:**
- Challenger panels have problems similar to Zinsco — some breaker models fail to trip under overload.
- Not as universally condemned as Zinsco or FPE, but many insurance companies flag them.
- Some Challenger models are considered acceptable; others are not. The specific concern is with certain breaker models that have documented failure issues.

**INTERLOCK COMPATIBILITY:**
- Some interlock kits (specifically GenInterlock/Siemens-type kits) list Challenger compatibility because Challenger's design is similar to Siemens/Murray in some respects.
- However, given safety concerns, Key should evaluate each Challenger panel individually.

**BPP ACTION:** Flag to Key — do not proceed without his evaluation. Some Challenger panels may be acceptable; others need replacement. Do not auto-qualify or auto-disqualify without Key's review.

---

### 2I. PUSHMATIC / BULLDOG PUSHMATIC

**VISUAL IDENTIFICATION:**
- Breaker type: PUSH BUTTONS instead of toggle switches. This is completely distinctive — there are no toggle/flip breakers. Each circuit has a rectangular pushbutton that you press to reset.
- Appearance: Buttons are typically tan/beige or brown. Heavy, industrial-looking panel.
- The brand name "Bulldog" or "Pushmatic" may be embossed in a circle on the panel cover or on a badge.
- Also branded as "ITE Pushmatic" in some installations.
- Panel age: Manufactured roughly 1950s–1980s.

**SAFETY STATUS:**
- Not as acutely dangerous as Zinsco or FPE, but these panels are old and the pushbutton mechanism can degrade.
- No trip-to-center visual indicator — harder to tell if a breaker has tripped.
- Replacement parts are scarce.

**INTERLOCK COMPATIBILITY:**
- Very limited or no interlock kit availability for Pushmatic panels.
- The pushbutton design doesn't interface with standard interlock kit sliding plate designs.

**BPP ACTION:** Flag to Key. Interlock installation is likely not feasible without panel replacement or a custom approach. These panels are rare but still appear in older Upstate SC homes.

---

### 2J. COMMANDER

- Commander is a less-common panel brand found occasionally in older manufactured homes and some budget residential installations.
- Visual identification: The "Commander" name on the panel cover/door. Breakers are typically black toggle-style.
- Some Commander panels used breakers that are compatible with Siemens/Murray-type designs.
- Interlock compatibility: Limited — not a mainstream brand, fewer kits available. Some Siemens-compatible kits may fit depending on specific panel design.
- **BPP ACTION:** Flag to Key for evaluation. Rare enough that specific guidance should come from Key rather than a general rule.

## SECTION 3: MLO (MAIN-LUG-ONLY) PANELS — VISUAL IDENTIFICATION

### WHAT IS AN MLO PANEL?

An MLO (Main-Lug-Only) panel has NO main breaker. Instead of a large 2-pole breaker at the top of the panel, it has large wire terminals (lugs) that accept the incoming service conductors directly. The bus bars are always energized when the utility is connected. There is NO local means of disconnecting all power within the panel itself.

---

### HOW TO VISUALLY IDENTIFY AN MLO PANEL

**When the panel cover/door is open:**

1. **No large breaker at the top.** In a main-breaker panel, you see one large 2-pole breaker (typically 100A, 150A, or 200A) at the very top, clearly larger than the branch circuit breakers below it. In an MLO, this space either has the incoming lugs exposed (covered by an insulating shroud or bus cap) OR has a short blank bus bar section.

2. **Look for "MAIN LUGS ONLY" label.** Many panels are labeled on the interior directory card or on the inside of the door. The label will often say "MAIN LUGS ONLY" or "MLO."

3. **Large wire terminals at the top of the bus bars.** You will see two heavy copper or aluminum lugs (one per hot leg) where the large incoming conductors (typically 4/0 or 350 kcmil aluminum) are clamped. These are significantly larger than any breaker terminal. They may be covered by an insulating cap or shroud.

4. **All breakers are branch circuit size.** There is no oversized breaker separate from the rest. The bus is full of uniformly sized branch breakers top-to-bottom.

**On the panel exterior:**
- The panel door/cover label may say "Main Lug" or "MLO."
- The spec label (inside or outside door) will state the panel's bus rating but will not list a main breaker amperage.

---

### WHERE IS THE DISCONNECT IN AN MLO CONFIGURATION?

In a residential MLO setup, the disconnect is ELSEWHERE:

1. **Outdoor meter-main combo:** The most common SC configuration. The meter socket and main breaker are in one outdoor enclosure (the "meter-main combo") mounted on the exterior of the house or on a pedestal. This feeds the indoor MLO panel via a feeder cable. The main breaker is the large 2-pole breaker in the outdoor combo unit.

2. **Upstream main breaker panel:** The MLO panel is a sub-panel. There is a main breaker panel elsewhere (often in the garage, basement, or utility room) that feeds the MLO as a downstream sub-panel.

3. **Separate disconnect switch:** Less common, but a standalone disconnect switch (a weatherproof enclosure with a large 2-pole breaker or fusible disconnect) may be mounted between the meter and the MLO panel.

4. **Service entrance equipment at the weatherhead/meter base:** In older installations, the utility meter base itself may have fuses (rare).

---

### WHY MLO MATTERS FOR INTERLOCK KIT INSTALLATION

This is critical for BPP:

- Standard interlock kits work by preventing the main breaker and the generator breaker from being ON simultaneously. They physically slide over both breakers.
- **If there is no main breaker in the panel, a standard interlock kit CANNOT BE INSTALLED in that panel.**
- For an MLO panel, the interlock must be installed at the location of the main disconnect (the outdoor meter-main combo or upstream panel), OR a dedicated generator breaker is added with a different approach.
- Alternatively, a 2-pole breaker can sometimes be added to the MLO panel to serve as the "utility feed" breaker, then interlocked against the generator breaker — but this requires Key's evaluation and varies by panel.

**BPP ACTION WHEN MLO IS IDENTIFIED:**
- Flag to Key immediately.
- Do NOT quote a standard interlock install until Key has evaluated the full service entrance configuration.
- In many SC homes, the MLO panel feeds from an outdoor meter-main — Key may install the interlock at the outdoor unit OR add a main breaker to the MLO panel.

---

### COMMON SC MLO CONFIGURATIONS

1. **New construction (2000s–present):** Outdoor meter-main combo on the exterior wall + indoor MLO panel. Extremely common in SC subdivisions. The main breaker is in the outdoor unit. The indoor panel is MLO. This is the standard configuration for SC homes built post-2000.

2. **Manufactured/mobile homes:** MLO panels are standard in manufactured housing. The disconnect is typically at an exterior pedestal or on the home's exterior wall. The interior panel is MLO.

3. **Sub-panels/shop panels:** Any time a second panel is added (detached garage, outbuilding, home addition), the downstream panel is MLO.

4. **Older split-bus panels:** Some older panels (1960s–1980s) have no single main breaker and instead use the "6-handle rule" — up to 6 large breakers in the upper section control the entire panel. These are not technically MLO (they have breakers, not lugs) but share the characteristic of having no single main disconnect. See Section 6 for split-bus details.

---

## SECTION 4: INTERLOCK KIT BRANDS AND COMPATIBILITY

### HOW INTERLOCK KITS WORK

An interlock kit is a mechanical sliding plate (usually aluminum or stainless steel) that mounts over the main breaker and the generator breaker in the panel. The plate is constrained so that BOTH breakers cannot be in the ON position simultaneously — if the main is ON, the generator breaker is physically blocked, and vice versa. This prevents backfeeding utility power through the generator (which would electrocute linemen working on the grid).

Interlock kits are panel-specific. They must match:
- Panel brand and product line (e.g., Square D QO vs. Homeline)
- Panel amperage (100A, 150A, 200A)
- Main breaker location (centered at top vs. offset to one side)
- Spacing between main breaker and first branch breaker (varies by panel model)

---

## SECTION 5: METER-MAIN COMBO PANELS — VISUAL IDENTIFICATION

### WHAT IS A METER-MAIN COMBO?

A meter-main combo (also called "meter-main," "all-in-one," or "combination service entrance") is a single enclosure that contains:
1. The utility meter socket (the round socket where the meter plugs in)
2. A main disconnect breaker (typically 100A, 150A, or 200A)
3. Sometimes: 4–40 branch circuit breaker spaces (in larger units)

When the combo unit has breaker spaces, it can serve as the ONLY panel in the home. When it has only the meter socket and main breaker (no branch spaces), it feeds a separate indoor MLO sub-panel.

---

### VISUAL APPEARANCE

**Exterior/Outdoor Mounting:**
- Almost always mounted OUTSIDE on the exterior wall of the home, a garage, or on a separate weatherproof pedestal/post.
- The enclosure is a NEMA 3R (weatherproof) painted metal box, typically GRAY, though some are tan or beige.
- Significantly TALLER than a standard indoor panel — the meter socket section adds considerable height.
- The meter socket is clearly visible: a round glass or polycarbonate dome (the actual meter) snapping into a circular socket. On "ringless" meter sockets, there is no ring around the meter dome; on "ring-type" sockets, there is a threaded ring that locks the meter in place.
- There is a MAIN BREAKER visible when the panel door is opened — this is typically a large 2-pole breaker at the top of the breaker section.
- The overall visual impression is of a taller, heavier outdoor enclosure compared to a standard indoor load center.

**Sizing:**
- Common residential sizes: 8-circuit (small, for limited branch circuits), 16-circuit, 20-circuit, 40-circuit.
- Amperage ratings: 100A, 150A, 200A (most common in residential SC), 320A, 400A (larger commercial/residential).

**How it differs from a standard indoor panel:**
| Feature | Meter-Main Combo | Indoor Load Center |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Location | Outside (NEMA 3R rated) | Inside (NEMA 1 rated) |
| Enclosure | Gray weatherproof metal, taller | Gray painted metal, flatter |
| Meter socket | YES — dome/meter visible | NO meter socket |
| Main breaker | YES — inside combo | Often YES, but varies (may be MLO) |
| Separate meter base | Not needed — built-in | Separate outdoor meter base required |

---

### COMMON BRANDS

- **Square D Homeline** outdoor meter-main combos (most common in SC residential)
- **Eaton BR** outdoor meter-main combos
- **Siemens** outdoor meter-main combos
- **Leviton** outdoor meter-main combos (newer construction)
- **Milbank** (meter socket manufacturer — common in the Carolinas for utility-approved meter bases)
- **Eaton / Thomas & Betts** (for manufactured homes)

---

### WHEN METER-MAINS ARE USED IN SC RESIDENTIAL

**Manufactured/Mobile Homes:**
- Standard practice. The meter-main is on a pedestal at the road or on the home's exterior. The interior panel is MLO. South Carolina Manufactured Housing Board regulations require a disconnect at or near the meter; the meter-main combo satisfies this.

**New Construction (post-2000, especially subdivisions):**
- Extremely common in Upstate SC. Builder-grade homes almost universally use an outdoor meter-main combo (typically 200A, 8–20 spaces) on the exterior wall, feeding an indoor MLO panel.
- This configuration is common in the Southeast specifically because:
  - Fire departments require an accessible outdoor disconnect
  - SC code and utility (Duke Energy Carolinas, Dominion Energy SC, etc.) requirements favor it
  - Climate — keeping the full breaker panel indoors prevents moisture/corrosion; the combo unit handles weather
  - Wall space in newer home designs often doesn't accommodate a large indoor panel near the service entrance

**Older Homes (1960s–1980s):**
- Less common — these homes often have a separate outdoor meter base + an indoor main-breaker panel.
- If you see an older home with a meter-main combo, it was likely a service upgrade.

**Homes on Well/Septic (Rural SC):**
- Meter-main on a treated wood or metal pedestal near the road or driveway, with a long underground feeder to the house. Common in rural Upstate SC and Anderson, Oconee, Pickens, Laurens county areas.

---

### INTERLOCK CONSIDERATIONS FOR METER-MAIN COMBOS

- If the meter-main has breaker spaces, Key can potentially install the generator breaker AND the interlock kit in the outdoor combo unit.
- If the meter-main has only the meter socket + main breaker (no branch spaces), the generator breaker must go in the indoor MLO panel, and the interlock approach is different.
- Outdoor installations require weatherproof inlet boxes (NEMA 3R rated) and weatherproof covers on all outlets.

## SECTION 8: GLOSSARY OF TERMS FOR ALEX

- **Inlet box / Power inlet:** The weatherproof outdoor receptacle (typically 30A L14-30R) mounted on the exterior of the home where the generator cord plugs in. This is what BPP installs.
- **Interlock kit:** The mechanical device installed in the panel that prevents the main breaker and generator breaker from being ON simultaneously.
- **Transfer switch:** A broader term for any device that transfers power between utility and generator. An interlock kit is a type of manual transfer switch.
- **Backfeed:** The dangerous condition where generator power flows backward through the meter into the utility grid. Interlock kits prevent this.
- **L14-30:** The 4-prong, 30A, 120/240V twist-lock outlet. The industry standard for connecting a generator to an inlet box via a 30A cord set.
- **Load center:** Another name for a breaker panel / electrical panel.
- **Service entrance:** The point where utility power enters the home, including the service conductors, meter base, and first disconnect.
- **Main breaker:** The large 2-pole breaker that can shut off all power to the panel at once. Typically 100A, 150A, or 200A.
- **MLO:** Main-Lug-Only. A panel with no main breaker — just lugs where incoming conductors terminate.
- **Branch circuit breakers:** The smaller individual breakers for each circuit in the home (15A for lights, 20A for kitchen/bath, 30A for dryer, etc.).
- **AFCI:** Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter. A modern type of breaker required in new construction for bedroom and living area circuits. Trips on arcing faults.
- **GFCI:** Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter. Required in wet areas (bathrooms, kitchen, outdoor outlets). Trips on ground faults.
- **Dual fuel:** A generator that runs on either gasoline or liquid propane (LPG). Does not affect outlet configuration.
- **Inverter generator:** Uses digital electronics to produce clean sine-wave power. Usually quieter and more fuel-efficient. Smaller inverters (EU2200i, RYi4022X, iGen4500) are often 120V only. Larger inverters (EU7000iS, Q6500) may have 240V.
- **Running watts vs. starting watts:** Running watts = continuous power capacity. Starting watts = brief surge for motor startup. BPP quotes based on running watts.
