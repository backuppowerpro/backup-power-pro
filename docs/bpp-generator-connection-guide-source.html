Why Most Generators Don't Actually Help During an Outage

The problem isn't the generator. It's the connection.

If you own a portable generator and it's sitting in your garage right now, you're not alone — and you're not as storm-ready as you think. Most generators in Upstate SC get used about twice a year: once when the homeowner fires it up to test it, and once when the power actually goes out and they scramble to figure out what to plug into it.

The Real Problem

Without a proper inlet and interlock installed at your electrical panel, your generator can't power your home's essential circuits — the lights on the wall switches, the refrigerator that's been running for years, the furnace blower that needs a thermostat to kick on, the well pump or sump pump that are wired directly into the house.

Running extension cords from a generator outside to appliances inside is not a connection. It's a workaround — and a dangerous one.

Extension cords create real risks:

✕
Backfeed hazard: Without an interlock, generator power can travel back into the utility lines — creating a lethal hazard for linemen working to restore power.
✕
Undersized cords: Most household extension cords aren't rated for the load a generator puts out. Overheated cords are a house fire waiting to happen.
✕
Code violation: This method violates NEC electrical code and is excluded from most homeowner insurance policies.
✕
CO risk: Running the generator too close to any opening to the home can allow carbon monoxide inside.

What you actually can't power:

Even with a generator running at full output, extension cords can't power:

  • Lights on wall switches
  • Refrigerator and freezer (wired circuits)
  • Well pump or sump pump
  • Furnace blower motor
  • HVAC systems
  • Garage door opener
  • Built-in appliances
The Bottom Line

Most homeowners who buy a generator spend $800–$2,000 on the machine and never make the $1,200–$1,500 connection that makes it actually work. This guide explains exactly what that connection is, what it costs, and what to look for before you hire anyone to do it.

What Actually Gets Installed — In Plain Language

Three components. One-day install. Code compliant from day one.

1

The Generator Inlet (Outdoor Receptacle)

A heavy-duty outdoor outlet mounted on the side of your home — near where your generator will be parked. This is what you plug your power cord into when the power goes out. It's weather-rated, tamper-resistant, and sized to the amperage of your generator. Looks clean — about the size of a dryer outlet on the exterior wall. This is the connection point that makes everything else possible.

2

The Interlock Kit (Mechanical Breaker Safety)

Installed directly inside your electrical panel. A metal slide mechanism that physically prevents your main utility breaker and your generator breaker from being on at the same time. This is what makes the whole system code-legal. Without an interlock — or a transfer switch — a generator can backfeed electricity into the utility lines and create a lethal hazard for the line workers trying to restore your power. The interlock is required by NEC code. It's not optional, and any electrician who skips it is cutting a dangerous corner.

3

The Power Cord

A heavy-gauge cord (typically 25 or 50 feet) that connects your generator to the inlet. Your generator stays outside — at least 20 feet from any openings to the home. The power runs through the cord, into the inlet, through the panel wiring, and out to the circuits you select. The cord is sized to your generator's output.

How You Use It During an Outage (6 Steps)

1

Move your generator outside — at least 20 feet from doors, windows, and vents

2

Add fuel if needed, start the generator, let it warm up for 30 seconds

3

Plug the power cord from the generator into the outdoor inlet on your home

4

Go to your breaker panel — slide the interlock to "generator mode" (this disconnects utility power)

5

Turn on the circuit breakers for the loads you want to power — refrigerator, lights, well pump, furnace, etc.

6

Your home runs on generator power through the circuits you selected

Worth Knowing

The whole process takes about 5 minutes once it's installed. There's nothing complicated about it — it's a panel connection you don't have yet. Once you have it, outages become an inconvenience instead of an emergency.

Permits, Inspections, and Why They Actually Protect You

This is the part most contractors don't explain. Here's the full picture.

Why a Permit Is Required in SC

In South Carolina, any electrical work involving new wiring, panel modifications, or the addition of a transfer switch or interlock must be permitted under NEC Article 702 (Optional Standby Systems). This applies across Greenville, Pickens, and Spartanburg counties. The permit triggers a county inspection, which verifies:

✓
The interlock is properly installed and mechanically sound
✓
The inlet is correctly rated for weather and amperage
✓
The wiring is correctly sized and properly terminated
✓
The installation is fully backfeed-protected

What Happens If You Skip the Permit

✕
Insurance exposure: Your homeowner's insurance may deny a claim related to generator damage or an electrical fire if the installation wasn't permitted and inspected.
✕
Home sale complications: A permitted installation adds value and sails through a buyer's home inspection. An unpermitted one can be flagged and require remediation — at your expense — before closing.
✕
Backfeed liability: If a utility worker is injured because of backfeed from an unpermitted installation, the liability exposure for the homeowner is significant.

What the Inspection Process Actually Looks Like

Once the install is complete, the electrician files the inspection request with the county. A county inspector — not the electrician — visits the home, reviews the work, and either passes it or asks for a correction. A properly done installation passes on the first visit, typically within a few business days of the install. You don't have to be present, but you can be. The passed inspection paperwork is yours to keep — put it with your homeowner's insurance docs.

Permit fees in the three-county area typically run $60–$120. That's included in a complete quote. An electrician who asks you to pay permit fees separately, or who quotes you a suspiciously low number that doesn't account for permit costs, is either passing the cost to you after the sale or planning to skip it.

5 Questions to Ask Before You Hire Anyone

Ask every electrician who quotes you. Any reputable contractor will answer yes to all five.

1

Are you a licensed SC Electrical Contractor?

Not just "licensed" — specifically a licensed electrical contractor in South Carolina under the SC Contractors' Licensing Board. Panel work requires this license by state law. Ask for the license number and verify it at contractors.sc.gov. Handyman licenses, general contractor licenses, and out-of-state licenses don't qualify for panel work.

2

Does your quote include the permit and county inspection?

If it doesn't — ask why. In Greenville, Pickens, and Spartanburg counties, permits are required and run $60–$120. An electrician who excludes this is either adding it to the invoice later or planning to skip it entirely. Neither is acceptable.

3

Have you installed this in Greenville, Pickens, or Spartanburg county before?

Each county's building department has slightly different inspection processes and inspector preferences. Experience in the local market matters — especially for first-pass inspection success. Ask how many installs they've done in the area.

4

Will you give me an all-in price before I commit?

A reputable electrician can give you a firm, complete quote after reviewing your setup: panel location, generator specs, and the distance from the panel to the install point. "I'll give you the final number once I'm on-site" is a red flag. You should know the full price before anyone touches your panel.

5

What warranty do you offer on the installation?

Hardware is covered by the manufacturer warranty. But the installation itself — the wiring quality, the mechanical fit of the interlock, the weatherproofing of the inlet — should be warranted by the contractor. If they won't stand behind their work, that tells you something about the work.
