Backup Power Pro 01 — Know Your Generator
Step one
What kind of generator
do you have?
Two things to find out before anything else. Look at your generator right now — the label on the side and the large outlet on the control panel.
Most common
30AMP
5,500 – 8,500 running watts
Found on most generators sold at Home Depot, Lowe's, and Costco. If you bought a name-brand portable generator in the last 10 years, there's a good chance this is what you have.
Outlet to look for: 4 prongs in a round housing — one prong is bent in an L-shape. Usually labeled L14-30. Twists to lock in place.
Larger units
50AMP
10,000 – 12,500 running watts
Found on larger generators, typically 10,000 watts and above. More headroom for bigger loads like central AC running alongside everything else.
Outlet to look for: 4 prongs, larger and spaced further apart than the 30A. May be a straight-blade outlet (looks like a dryer plug) or a twist-lock style. Usually labeled L14-50 or 14-50.
💡 Some generators have both outlets
If yours does, you have more flexibility. The inlet we install matches your generator — a 30A generator gets a 30A inlet, a 50A gets a 50A. You're never paying for more than you need.

❄️ A note on central AC
"Tight" means your generator may start a 2.5-ton central AC — but the startup surge (often 2–3× the running watts) is the risky part. Whether it works depends on your specific unit and your generator's surge rating. A device called a soft start (also called a hard start kit) installs on your AC compressor and reduces that startup spike by 50–70%, making it much more reliable. An HVAC technician can install one for $150–300 in parts and labor. Without it, we can't guarantee AC will start — even on a 50A system.
⚠ One thing to check before your quote

The inlet system requires a 240V outlet on your generator. Most generators have one — but some, especially smaller or older units, only output 120V. Here's how to know in 10 seconds:

✓ 4 prongs — you're good One of the bottom prongs is L-shaped (bent). This is the L14-30 or L14-50. It's a 240V outlet and works with the inlet system.
✗ 3 prongs — 120V only Even if it's labeled "30A," a 3-prong twist-lock (L5-30) or RV-style outlet (TT-30) runs on 120V only. The inlet system can't be installed with this generator.

🔥 Have gas appliances?
Gas water heater, dryer, and stove only use electricity for ignition and controls — roughly 150–400W total for all three. Those big loads essentially disappear from your list, freeing up most of your generator's capacity for everything else.
⛽ Natural gas generators
Some generators are designed to run on natural gas or propane instead of gasoline — no fuel storage, no stale fuel, runs as long as the line has pressure. If you have natural gas service at your home and want to explore this, a licensed plumber can run a gas line to where the generator would sit. It's a separate project from the inlet installation, but the two work together.
Your fridge, freezer, all the lights in your home, WiFi, TV, and every device charging — together that's roughly 800–1,200 watts. A 7,500-watt generator is running at about 15% capacity just covering all of that.

Backup Power Pro 04 — Safety
Step four
Running it safely
None of what's in this guide matters if the generator isn't run safely. Most of the rules are simple. One of them isn't optional.
⚠
Carbon Monoxide — the one that doesn't give second chances
CO is colorless and odorless. It builds up fast and incapacitates before you realize anything is wrong. A single portable generator produces up to 100 times more CO than a car exhaust (National Institute of Standards and Technology). Every year people die during outages not from running generators inside — but from running them just outside a window.
  • Never run a generator inside your home, garage, shed, or enclosed porch. Not with the door open. Not briefly. Never.
  • Keep it at least 20 feet from any door, window, or vent.
  • Point the exhaust away from your home — not sideways along a wall, not toward it.
  • Install working CO detectors on every level of your home. This matters beyond generators.
Running in wet weather
Generators aren't waterproof. A basic generator canopy from any hardware store ($30–50) protects it from rain. Whatever cover you use, make sure airflow around the unit isn't blocked — restricted airflow causes overheating.
Fuel & storage
Always shut the generator off and let it cool 2 minutes before refueling. Store gas in approved containers away from living spaces. If fuel will sit more than 30 days, add a stabilizer — stale fuel is the most common reason generators won't start when needed.
Extension cords
For a few hours, extension cords are fine. For days, they create problems — heat buildup in the cord insulation, voltage drop that damages sensitive electronics, and gaps in your home's envelope that let CO in. If you must use them: 12-gauge or heavier, outdoor-rated, as short as possible. Never coiled or run under a rug.
Keep it ready
Run your generator under load for 30 minutes every 90 days — not just start it, actually plug something in and let it work. This keeps the fuel system clean and tells you before a storm whether anything needs attention. A generator that won't start during a storm is worse than not having one.

Backup Power Pro 05 — Permits & Protection
Step five
Why the permit matters
to you personally
Some contractors skip the permit. Here's what that decision actually costs the homeowner — not the contractor.
🏠 Your insurance
Homeowner's policies include clauses about unpermitted electrical modifications. If an unpermitted installation is connected to any claim — fire, storm damage, anything electrical — your insurer has documented grounds to reduce or deny it. A permitted, inspected installation removes that exposure entirely.
💰 Your home's value
Unpermitted electrical work shows up in buyer inspections. It becomes a negotiating point, a required repair, or a deal-breaker. A permitted, inspected installation is documented as a home improvement that transfers cleanly to the next owner and adds to the sale.
👷 Real safety check
The inspection verifies that the interlock mechanism works correctly — the device that physically prevents your generator and the grid from connecting simultaneously. That protects your family, and it protects utility workers restoring power in your neighborhood who could be harmed by electricity feeding back into lines they believe are dead.
⚡ One technical note about your generator

Most portable generators come from the factory with the neutral and ground wires bonded together inside the unit. That's correct for standalone use with extension cords. But when a generator is connected to your home's panel through an inlet, your panel already has its own neutral-ground bond. Two bond points creates a code violation (NEC 250.34) and will cause GFCI outlets to trip randomly — and in some cases puts current on ground wires that are supposed to be dead.

The fix is called a floating neutral — the bond inside the generator is removed or disconnected. Many manufacturers make this accessible: it may be a removable bonding plug, a single wire in the control panel, or a documented procedure in your owner's manual. Search your model + "floating neutral" or "neutral bond removal" — most major brands have instructions. If you'd rather not do it yourself, any small engine repair shop can handle it in under an hour.
