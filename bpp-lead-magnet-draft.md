## PART 1: WHAT MAKES AN EFFECTIVE HOME SERVICE LEAD MAGNET

### The Core Psychology

A lead magnet for a home service business has one job before it has a conversion job: it has to make the recipient feel like they got something real. The failure mode is a PDF that reads like a brochure pretending to be content. When homeowners receive that, they feel manipulated, not helped, and trust drops before it ever forms.

The best home service lead magnets share these traits:

**1. They solve a problem the customer already knows they have.**
Not a problem the business thinks they have. A homeowner who filled out a generator form already knows their situation. They don't need to be sold on the concept. They want specifics, clarity, and a roadmap.

**2. They are scannable, not readable.**
The PDF will be opened on a phone, probably standing in a kitchen. Headers, bold callouts, short paragraphs, and visual checklists are the format. Wall-to-wall text kills engagement in 10 seconds.

**3. They are local and specific.**
"South Carolina" and "Upstate SC" in the copy signals to the reader that this isn't a generic download — this person knows my area. That specificity dramatically increases perceived credibility.

**4. They position expertise without being braggy.**
A licensed electrician explaining what to watch out for, what questions to ask, what the county requires — that is demonstrating expertise. Saying "We are the best" is not.

**5. They answer the question the reader actually has right now.**
For BPP's audience, the real question is usually one of these:
- "I already own a generator. What do I need to actually use it?"
- "Is this worth the money? What do I actually get?"
- "What could go wrong if I skip the professional installation?"
- "What's the difference between what BPP does vs. a standby generator?"

**6. They end with a natural next step — not a hard sell.**
The CTA should feel like "here's the obvious thing to do now that you have this information," not "BUY NOW."

### Format Principles for Home Service PDFs

- **Length:** 6–10 pages is ideal. Long enough to feel substantial. Short enough to actually be read.
- **Cover page:** Strong headline, brand identity, professional appearance = immediate credibility signal.
- **Reading time indicator:** "8-minute read" or "5 pages" on the cover sets expectations and reduces drop-off.
- **One clear CTA per page:** Not multiple competing asks. Each page leads somewhere.
- **Final page = soft offer:** The last page earns the right to make an ask because the prior pages earned trust.

### PAGE 4: HOW A GENERATOR INLET AND INTERLOCK ACTUALLY WORKS

**Purpose:** Demystify the installation. Remove the "I don't understand what I'm buying" objection.

**Section title:** "What Actually Gets Installed — In Plain Language"

**The three components (numbered list with brief explanation of each):**

1. **The Generator Inlet (outdoor receptacle)**
   A heavy-duty outdoor outlet, typically mounted on the side of the home near where the generator will be parked. This is what you plug your generator's power cord into. Weather-rated, tamper-resistant, and rated for the amperage of your generator. Looks clean — about the size of a dryer outlet.

2. **The Interlock Kit (mechanical breaker safety)**
   Installed directly on your electrical panel. A metal slide mechanism that physically prevents the main utility breaker and the generator breaker from being on at the same time. This is what makes the installation code-legal. Without an interlock (or transfer switch), a generator can backfeed electricity into the utility lines — creating a lethal hazard for utility workers restoring power. Required by NEC code.

3. **The Power Cord**
   A heavy-gauge cord (typically 25 or 50 feet) that connects your generator to the inlet. The generator stays outside. The power runs through the cord into the inlet, through the panel, and out to your selected circuits.

**How you use it during an outage (numbered steps — short and simple):**
1. Move your generator outside (away from windows and doors — 20 feet minimum)
2. Add fuel if needed, start the generator
3. Plug the power cord from the generator into the outdoor inlet
4. Go to your breaker panel, flip the interlock to "generator mode"
5. Turn on the circuits you want to power (fridge, lights, well pump, etc.)
6. Your home runs on generator power through the circuits you selected

**Callout box:** "The whole process takes about 5 minutes once it's installed. There's nothing complicated about it — it's just a panel connection that you don't have yet."

### PAGE 6: THE PERMIT AND INSPECTION — WHAT SC HOMEOWNERS NEED TO KNOW

**Purpose:** Demonstrate technical/local expertise. Build confidence that BPP knows the regulatory landscape.

**Section title:** "Permits, Inspections, and Why They Actually Protect You"

**Why a permit is required:**
In South Carolina, any electrical work that involves new wiring, panel modifications, or the addition of a transfer switch or interlock must be permitted under NEC Article 702 (Optional Standby Systems). This applies to Greenville, Pickens, and Spartanburg counties. The permit triggers a county inspection, which verifies:
- The interlock is properly installed and mechanically sound
- The inlet is properly rated and weather-protected
- The wiring is correctly sized and terminated
- The installation is backfeed-protected

**What happens if you skip the permit:**
- Your homeowner's insurance may deny a claim related to generator damage or a generator-caused electrical fire
- If you sell your home, a permitted installation adds value; an unpermitted one can be flagged in the inspection and require remediation
- If a utility worker is injured because of backfeed from an unpermitted installation, the liability exposure is significant

**What the inspection process looks like:**
Once the install is complete, the electrician files the inspection request with the county. An inspector (county employee, not the electrician) visits, reviews the work, and either passes or asks for a correction. A properly done installation passes on the first visit. The homeowner doesn't have to be present, but can be. The paperwork is yours to keep.

### PAGE 7: HOW TO CHOOSE A GENERATOR ELECTRICIAN IN UPSTATE SC

**Purpose:** Give the reader a smart-buyer checklist. This is the most trust-building section in the guide because it arms them with questions that any good electrician (including BPP) will pass easily.

**Section title:** "5 Questions to Ask Before You Hire Anyone"

Present as a numbered list — clean, authoritative, useful:

1. **Are you a licensed SC Electrical Contractor?**
   Not just "licensed" — specifically a licensed electrical contractor in South Carolina. This is the SC Contractors' Licensing Board license (e.g., "LIC #XXXX"), not a handyman license. This is required by state law for panel work. Ask for the license number and verify it at contractors.sc.gov.

2. **Does your quote include the permit and county inspection?**
   If not — ask why. In the three-county area, permits are required and typically cost $60–$120. An electrician who excludes this is either passing the cost to you later or skipping it entirely.

3. **Have you installed this type of system in Greenville/Pickens/Spartanburg county before?**
   Each county has slightly different inspection processes and inspector preferences. Experience in the local market matters. Ask how many installs they've done.

4. **Will you give me an all-in price before I commit?**
   A reputable electrician can and should give you a firm quote after reviewing your setup (panel location, generator specs, distance from panel to outdoor install point). "I'll give you the final number once I'm on-site" is a red flag.

5. **What warranty do you offer on the installation?**
   The workmanship should be warranted. Hardware is covered by manufacturer warranty, but the installation itself — the wiring, the mechanical interlock fit, the weatherproofing — should be guaranteed by the contractor.
