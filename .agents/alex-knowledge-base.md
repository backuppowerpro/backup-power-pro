## TOPIC 1: SOUTH CAROLINA ELECTRICAL PERMIT & INSPECTION PROCESS

### Who Issues Permits?

South Carolina does NOT issue electrical permits at the state level for this type of work. Permits are issued at the **local level** — either by the **county** or by a **municipality** depending on where the property is located:

- **Greenville County** (unincorporated areas): Greenville County Building Safety Department
  301 University Ridge, Suite 4100, Greenville SC 29601 | Portal: eTRAKiT system
- **City of Greenville** (inside city limits): City of Greenville Building Permit Center
- **City of Mauldin, Simpsonville, Greer, Fountain Inn, Travelers Rest**: Each city has its own building/permits department
- **Spartanburg County** (unincorporated areas): Spartanburg County Building Codes | Portal: EnerGov / Citizen Self Service
- **City of Spartanburg** (inside city limits): City of Spartanburg Building Inspections
- **Pickens County** (all areas including Easley, Clemson, Liberty, Central): Pickens County Building Codes Administration
  222 McDaniel Avenue, B-10, Pickens SC 29671 | Phone: (864) 898-5950 | Portal: EnerGov

The **SC LLR (Department of Labor, Licensing & Regulation)** does not issue permits but licenses the electricians who pull them. Key holds an active electrical contractor license through SC LLR, which is required to pull permits in any SC jurisdiction.

SC adopted the **2020 National Electrical Code (NEC)** as its statewide baseline. Local jurisdictions may adopt local amendments on top of this.

### What Information Is Required on the Permit Application?

A typical SC county electrical permit application for generator inlet work requires:
- **Property address** (street address, city, zip, parcel number)
- **Property owner name and contact information**
- **Electrical contractor name, license number, and contact info** (Key's SC license number must appear)
- **Description of work** — "Install generator inlet box, 30A 240V circuit, interlock kit on main panel"
- **Estimated project valuation** (typically $1,200–$2,000 for a residential inlet install)
- **Panel information** — panel brand, amperage, number of breakers
- **Number of circuits being added** (typically one new 30A 240V double-pole breaker)

Some counties (Greenville) charge permit fees based on project valuation. Fees typically range from **$50–$150** for a residential generator inlet installation. Greenville County increased fees 20% effective July 1, 2025.

### The SC Inspection Framework

South Carolina uses a **local Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)** model. The county or city inspector is the final word. There is no separate SC LLR inspection for residential electrical work — the county inspector handles it.

For a generator inlet installation, there is typically **one inspection: the final inspection** after all work is complete. No rough-in inspection is required because the new wiring runs from an exterior inlet box to the main panel — there is nothing hidden inside walls that needs a rough-in check.

### Inspection Timeline by County

| County | Permit Processing | Inspection Scheduling | Total Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Greenville County | 2–3 weeks (eTRAKiT submission) | Next business day if requested before 6:30 AM | 3–5 weeks typical |
| City of Greenville | Varies; typically 1–2 weeks | Next business day | 2–4 weeks typical |
| Spartanburg County | Typically 1–2 weeks | Must request before 6:30 AM for next-day | 2–4 weeks typical |
| Pickens County | Typically 1–2 weeks | Call (864) 898-5950 | 1–3 weeks typical |

**What this means for customers**: Key pulls the permit before the install day. After installation, the inspector comes out — typically within a few days to 2 weeks. The customer does not need to be present for the inspection in most cases, though some counties require the contractor to be available. Once the inspection passes, the job is complete and closed out.

### What Does the Inspector Actually Check?

The inspector verifies compliance with NEC 2020 and local amendments. For a generator inlet installation, they typically check:

1. **Interlock device** — physically prevents main breaker and generator breaker from being ON simultaneously. This is the core code requirement under NEC Article 702 (Optional Standby Systems).
2. **Wire sizing** — the conductors from inlet box to panel must be sized correctly for the breaker (10 AWG for 30A, 8 AWG preferred by many inspectors for a longer run).
3. **Breaker sizing** — the generator breaker must match the inlet box amperage rating (30A breaker for 30A inlet).
4. **Inlet box weatherproofing** — must be a listed, weatherproof inlet box rated for outdoor use (typically a Reliance or Leviton inlet with a flip-up cover).
5. **Conduit installation** — any conduit run must be properly supported, LB fittings used at bends, conduit appropriate type for the environment (often EMT or Schedule 40 PVC).
6. **Grounding and bonding** — proper grounding of the inlet box and conduit system.
7. **Labeling** — NEC 702.7 requires a caution label on or near the panel indicating it is connected to an optional standby system and proper operating sequence.
8. **Panel integrity** — the panel cover is reinstalled correctly, knockouts are properly filled, no open holes.
9. **Permit card/placard** — must be posted at the job site.

### Common Reasons a Generator Inlet Installation Fails Inspection

1. **Wrong wire gauge** — undersized conductors for the breaker size
2. **Missing interlock label** — NEC 702.7 requires a caution/operating sequence label; easy to forget
3. **Conduit not properly supported** — conduit straps missing or too far apart
4. **Open knockouts in panel** — any unused panel knockout left open after work is a violation
5. **Inlet box not weatherproof** — using an indoor-rated box on an exterior wall
6. **No strain relief** on conduit entering panel
7. **Breaker size mismatch** — 50A breaker on 10 AWG wire, or 30A inlet with 40A breaker
8. **Neutral/ground separation issue** — rare in a simple interlock job but possible if panel wiring is disturbed
9. **No permit pulled** — the most common issue when DIY or unlicensed work is done

## TOPIC 2: GENERATOR PLACEMENT REQUIREMENTS & SAFETY

### The Governing Code: NFPA 37 vs. Manufacturer Guidelines vs. CPSC

Three separate standards govern generator placement:

| Standard | Applies To | Key Distance Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **NFPA 37** | Permanently installed standby generators | 5 feet from wall openings |
| **NEC Article 702** | Electrical connection to building | No placement distance — electrical code only |
| **CPSC Guidance** | Consumer safety for portable generators | 20 feet from home |
| **Manufacturer manuals** (Honda, Generac, Champion, etc.) | Portable generators | Typically 20 feet from openings |

**For Backup Power Pro's customers** (portable generators connected via inlet box), the applicable standard is the **CPSC/manufacturer guidance of 20 feet** — not NFPA 37, which applies to permanently installed standby units.

### Minimum Distances: Portable Generator Placement

- **From any door or window**: Minimum 20 feet (manufacturer/CPSC recommendation)
- **From HVAC air intake, dryer vent, bathroom exhaust vent, kitchen vent**: Minimum 20 feet — exhaust from these openings can pull CO into the home
- **From gas meters**: Minimum 5 feet (and never point exhaust toward a gas meter)
- **From LP/propane tanks**: Minimum 5 feet; never direct exhaust toward a tank
- **From property lines**: No universal code requirement, but many municipalities require 5–10 feet; check local ordinances
- **Exhaust direction**: Always point the exhaust AWAY from the house and away from any neighbor's windows or vents

**Never operate a portable generator in any of these locations**:
- Inside the home
- In an attached garage (even with door open — CO penetrates interior walls and door gaps)
- In a detached garage (even with door open — CO accumulates and can reach lethal levels)
- On a covered porch or patio
- In a basement or crawlspace
- Under a deck or overhang that traps exhaust

### Carbon Monoxide: The Real Numbers

CO is the leading cause of portable generator deaths in the United States.

- **CPSC data**: Approximately 85–100 consumers die each year in the U.S. from CO poisoning caused by portable generators
- **81% of fatalities** occur in residential settings
- **50% of fatalities** occur in November through February (storm season overlap)
- CO is colorless, odorless, and tasteless — there is NO warning before incapacitation
- A 5,000-watt generator produces roughly the same CO as **700 idling cars**
- Dangerous CO levels (200+ ppm) can accumulate inside a home within **minutes** of a generator running in an attached garage — even with the garage door open
- Lethal CO levels (800+ ppm) can be reached inside a home in under 10 minutes with a generator in an attached garage

### Practical Placement Recommendation for Upstate SC Homeowners

Key's standard customer guidance:
1. Place the generator on a hard, level surface (concrete driveway, pavers, compacted gravel) — never on wet grass or soft ground that can shift
2. At least 20 feet from any door, window, or vent — this is non-negotiable
3. Point the exhaust sideways or away from the home (not toward a neighbor's house either)
4. The generator power cord runs from the generator across the yard to the inlet box on the exterior wall — cord management (a good 30A L14-30 cord rated for the distance) is part of the install discussion
5. In Upstate SC, most homes have a driveway or concrete slab that naturally puts the generator at 20+ feet from the back door — this works well
6. Keep a CO detector inside the home near sleeping areas — it is code in new construction and strongly recommended for all homes using a generator

## TOPIC 3: CIRCUIT PRIORITIZATION — WHAT TO RUN ON A GENERATOR

### The 30A 240V System Context

When BPP installs a 30A 240V inlet box and interlock kit, the generator's power is connected to the entire main panel. The homeowner controls which circuits draw power by manually managing breakers. A typical 7,500W–10,000W portable generator delivers roughly **7,500–10,000 running watts** — this is the budget the homeowner works within.

**Critical rule**: The total running wattage of all ON circuits must not exceed the generator's continuous rated wattage. The generator's surge/peak rating handles motor startups (refrigerators, pumps, AC compressors) for a few seconds only.

### Essential Load Wattage Reference

| Appliance | Running Watts | Startup Surge | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Refrigerator/freezer | 150–800W | 1,200–1,600W | Runs in cycles; averages ~400W |
| Chest freezer | 100–500W | 600–1,200W | Very efficient; high priority |
| Sump pump (1/3 HP) | 800W | 1,300–2,900W | Critical in Upstate SC flooding events |
| Sump pump (1/2 HP) | 1,050W | 2,150–4,100W | Higher surge; start before other loads |
| Well pump (3/4 HP, 240V) | 750–1,000W | 2,000–3,000W | Highest single surge load; start first |
| Well pump (1 HP, 240V) | 1,000–1,500W | 3,000–4,500W | Requires a larger generator |
| CPAP (basic, no heat) | 30–60W | Minimal | Very low draw; always run this |
| CPAP (with heated humidifier) | 100–200W | Minimal | Still manageable |
| Oxygen concentrator (home) | 300–600W | 600–1,200W | High medical priority; verify wattage |
| LED lighting (10 circuits) | 50–200W total | Minimal | Very low draw; always run |
| Phone/device charging | 20–100W total | Minimal | Negligible |
| Internet router + modem | 20–50W | Minimal | Always run; low draw |
| Garage door opener | 300–500W | 700–1,200W | Convenient but not essential |
| Window AC (5,000 BTU) | 450–900W | 1,200–1,800W | Manageable on most generators |
| Window AC (12,000 BTU) | 900–1,440W | 2,400–3,800W | Feasible with soft start |
| Central AC (2-ton, 240V) | 2,500W | 6,000–8,000W | Startup surge is the challenge |
| Central AC (3-ton, 240V) | 3,500W | 8,000–12,000W | Requires large generator or soft start |
| Electric water heater (240V) | 4,500–5,500W | Minimal (resistive) | NOT practical on most generators |
| Electric range/oven (240V) | 5,000–14,400W | Minimal (resistive) | NOT practical on most generators |
| Tankless electric water heater | 10,000–30,000W | High | Never on a portable generator |
| Electric dryer (240V) | 5,000–7,000W | Minimal (resistive) | NOT practical; use clothesline |
| Electric furnace/air handler | 5,000–25,000W | High | NOT practical on portable generator |

### Circuit Prioritization — Recommended Order

**Tier 1 — Always On (essential)**:
1. Refrigerator and chest freezer (food safety)
2. Medical equipment (CPAP, oxygen concentrator, insulin storage refrigerator)
3. Well pump or sump pump (water access and flood prevention)
4. Lighting (LED circuits are low draw)
5. Internet router/modem and phone charging
6. Furnace blower motor (gas furnace only — the blower motor is typically 400–800W; the gas burner doesn't use electricity)

**Tier 2 — Run as capacity allows**:
7. Window AC unit or one zone of mini-split (summer storms)
8. Garage door opener (convenience, security)
9. TV and entertainment (minimal draw)

**Tier 3 — Do NOT run on a standard portable generator**:
- Electric water heater (240V, 4,500–5,500W continuous)
- Electric range/oven/cooktop (240V, 5,000–14,400W)
- Tankless electric water heater
- Electric dryer
- Electric furnace
- Electric vehicle charger
- Hot tub or pool heater
- Any 240V appliance that is purely resistive and draws 3,000W+ continuously

**Practical Note**: Running a gas range or gas water heater on generator power is a non-issue — only the igniter (minimal) uses electricity on most gas appliances.

### Load Management Sequence (Well Pump Homes)

For homes with a 240V well pump, Key's recommended startup sequence:
1. Start the generator, let it warm up 2–3 minutes at no load
2. Turn OFF all non-essential circuit breakers at the panel
3. Flip the main breaker OFF, engage the interlock, flip the generator breaker ON
4. Turn ON the well pump breaker first (largest motor — highest surge, needs clean power)
5. Wait 30 seconds, then turn on refrigerator
6. Wait 30 seconds, then turn on other circuits one at a time
7. Never try to start all motors simultaneously

### Central AC and the Soft Start Solution

The challenge with central AC is the compressor startup surge: a 2.5-ton unit that runs at 2,800W typically surges to 6,000–8,000W for 1–3 seconds at startup. Most 7,500W generators cannot handle this.

**Soft start devices** (MicroAir EasyStart, SoftStart Home) solve this by electronically ramping the compressor motor voltage gradually instead of slamming it with full power at once. Benefits:
- Reduce startup current by **60–75%**
- A 2.5-ton AC that normally surges to 7,000W may only surge to 2,500W with a soft start
- Allows a 7,500W generator to run a standard 2.5-ton central AC
- Typical soft start device cost: **$150–$400**
- Installation is at the AC condenser unit (outside), typically a 30-minute add-on job
- The MicroAir EasyStart Flex is the most commonly used product for residential central AC

**When a customer asks about running central AC**: The honest answer is "it depends on your AC size and generator size — but we can add a soft start device that makes it work on your existing generator in most cases."

## TOPIC 4: WHAT INSTALL DAY LOOKS LIKE (CUSTOMER-FACING)

### Before Install Day

- Key pulls the permit before scheduling the install. This is included in the price — no extra fees.
- Key contacts the customer to confirm arrival time, typically morning (8–10 AM)
- Customer should know their panel location (basement, garage, utility room, exterior)
- Customer should know approximately where they want the inlet box located (typically near where they'll park the generator — side of house, back of house near driveway)
- Customer does NOT need to prepare anything special

### Arrival and Assessment (15–20 minutes)

- Key arrives at the agreed time, introduces himself, does a brief walkthrough
- Examines the main electrical panel: brand, available breaker slots, current wiring
- Walks the exterior with the customer to finalize inlet box location — considers:
  - Proximity to generator parking spot
  - Avoidance of foundation plantings or obstructions
  - Length of conduit run (shorter = better)
  - Distance from utility meter and gas lines
- Confirms the install plan with the customer before starting any work

### The Installation (1.5–3 hours total)

Key's standard installation sequence:

**Step 1 — Mount the inlet box** (30–45 min)
- Marks the exterior wall location
- Drills through the exterior wall/rim joist for the conduit entry
- Mounts the weatherproof inlet box (typically a Reliance 30A L14-30 inlet or equivalent) using weatherproof screws and exterior sealant around the base
- The inlet has a flip-up cover that protects it from weather when not in use

**Step 2 — Run the conduit** (20–40 min)
- Runs conduit (typically Schedule 40 PVC or EMT) from the inlet box to a penetration point into the home
- Routes through the rim joist/basement wall/crawlspace to the panel location
- Properly supports conduit with straps at required intervals
- Uses LB conduit body at any 90-degree bends on the exterior

**Step 3 — Pull wire** (15–20 min)
- Pulls 10 AWG THHN/THWN-2 (or 8 AWG depending on run length) through the conduit
- Hot/hot/neutral/ground (four conductors) for 240V circuit
- Leaves proper service loops at both ends

**Step 4 — Install the breaker** (15–20 min)
- De-energizes the panel (shuts off main breaker, confirms dead with meter)
- Installs the new 30A double-pole circuit breaker in the designated slot
- Lands the wires: two hots to breaker terminals, neutral to neutral bar, ground to ground bar
- Labels the breaker "GENERATOR" with a permanent label

**Step 5 — Install the interlock kit** (20–30 min)
- Installs the panel-specific interlock device (brand/model specific — Key stocks the correct kits for common panels in Upstate SC)
- Attaches the sliding interlock plate that physically prevents the main breaker and generator breaker from both being ON
- Tests the interlock — confirms it slides correctly, blocks simultaneous operation, moves freely
- Reinstalls panel cover

**Step 6 — Final connections and sealing** (15 min)
- Terminates wiring at the inlet box
- Applies exterior sealant at all wall penetrations (conduit entry, inlet box base)
- Applies required NEC 702.7 caution label to the panel cover

### The Walkthrough (15–20 minutes) — THIS IS CRITICAL

Before leaving, Key walks the customer through exactly how to use the system. This is a core part of the BPP experience.

Key covers:
1. **The outlet on the generator**: Where to plug in the power cord (L14-30 4-prong outlet)
2. **The power cord**: Usually a 25-foot to 50-foot L14-30 generator cord (customer's existing cord or available for purchase) — how to connect it to the inlet box
3. **The interlock operation — step by step**:
   - Go to the main panel
   - Turn OFF the main breaker
   - Slide the interlock plate to block the main breaker position
   - Turn ON the generator breaker
   - Go start the generator
   - Let it warm up 2–3 minutes
   - Connect the cord to the inlet
   - Turn on circuits one at a time
4. **Circuit selection**: Which breakers to turn on and which to leave off (the "don't touch these" list — electric water heater, electric range, etc.)
5. **Restoration sequence** (when utility power returns):
   - Disconnect the cord from the inlet
   - Let the generator cool down 5 minutes
   - At the panel: turn OFF the generator breaker
   - Slide the interlock to unblock the main breaker position
   - Turn ON the main breaker
   - Normal power resumes
6. **Generator placement reminder**: 20 feet away, exhaust away from house
7. **CO detector recommendation**: Where to put it, what to do if it alarms

### What the Site Looks Like When Done

- Exterior wall: A mounted, weatherproof inlet box with a flip-up cover — clean and professional
- Conduit: Properly run, supported, and sealed — no exposed wires
- Interior: No visible changes except the new breaker labeled "GENERATOR" and the interlock device on the panel
- No open wall holes, no debris, no leftover materials
- Key cleans up all debris, takes all scrap materials with him

### Paperwork the Customer Receives

## TOPIC 5: TRANSFER SWITCH vs. INTERLOCK KIT — CUSTOMER QUESTIONS

### The Three Options: Clear Explanation

**Option 1: Generator Interlock Kit (what BPP installs)**

A mechanical metal plate that physically prevents the main utility breaker and the generator breaker from being ON at the same time. It is installed directly on the existing main electrical panel. No separate subpanel, no electronics, no moving parts except the sliding plate.

- **Operation**: Manual. Customer turns off main breaker, slides the interlock, turns on generator breaker.
- **Panel access**: Connects to ALL circuits in the home — customer chooses which circuits to run by managing individual breakers.
- **Cost**: Interlock kit hardware: $50–$150. Full installed cost from BPP: included in the $1,400–$2,000 total install package.
- **Code compliance**: Legal and NEC-compliant under NEC Article 702 (Optional Standby Systems) when installed by a licensed electrician with a permit.
- **Reliability**: Extremely high — no electronics to fail, no sensors.
- **Best for**: Homeowners who want whole-panel access, maximum flexibility, lowest cost, and are comfortable with a simple manual procedure during an outage.

**Option 2: Manual Transfer Switch (subpanel type)**

A separate small subpanel (typically 6–10 circuits) wired with a rotary or toggle switch that manually switches those circuits between utility power and generator power. Common brands: Reliance Controls, Generac.

- **Operation**: Manual. Customer goes to the transfer switch box and flips each circuit's toggle or rotary switch.
- **Panel access**: LIMITED to only the circuits wired into the transfer switch subpanel.
- **Cost**: Switch hardware: $200–$400. Installed cost: typically $800–$1,500 installed (more labor due to subpanel wiring).
- **Code compliance**: NEC compliant; uses a physically separate means of transfer rather than the main panel.
- **Best for**: Homes where the main panel has no available space for a breaker, or where a simple dedicated circuit solution is preferred.
- **Downside**: Customer pays for a new subpanel and only gets power to the pre-wired circuits — less flexible than an interlock.

**Option 3: Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS)**

An electronic device that detects a utility power outage, signals the generator to start (if a standby generator with electric start), and automatically switches the home to generator power — all without any manual action. Reverses automatically when utility power returns.

- **Operation**: Fully automatic. Zero manual steps during an outage.
- **Panel access**: Depends on model — can be whole-panel or selected circuits.
- **Cost**: Hardware: $600–$2,000+. Installed cost: typically $1,500–$4,000+.
- **Code compliance**: NEC Article 702; must be installed by a licensed electrician.
- **Catch**: Only works automatically if paired with a generator that has **electric start and remote start capability**. Most portable generators customers already own are pull-start only — an ATS does NOT make a manual-start portable generator automatic.
- **Best for**: Standby generator systems ($15,000+ whole-home generators) or portable generators with remote start capability when the homeowner is willing to pay for the convenience.

### Why Most BPP Customers Choose the Interlock Kit

1. **Price**: Interlock is included in the BPP package. An ATS adds $1,500–$4,000+ to the cost with minimal practical benefit for a portable generator owner.
2. **Their generator won't auto-start anyway**: The customer already owns a pull-start portable generator. An ATS can't make a manual-start generator automatic — they'd still have to go outside, pull the cord, and connect the cord to the inlet.
3. **Whole-panel access**: The interlock connects to ALL circuits; a manual transfer switch subpanel only covers the pre-selected circuits.
4. **Simplicity and reliability**: No electronics to fail. The interlock plate is a simple mechanical device that will work in 20 years exactly as it does today.
5. **Code and insurance friendly**: Installed with a permit and licensed electrician — fully code compliant and insurable.

### What If a Customer Already Has an ATS?

If a customer already has an automatic transfer switch installed (from a prior standby generator setup), Key can assess whether it can be repurposed or whether a simpler inlet-and-interlock approach makes more sense given their current portable generator. This is an assessment Key handles case by case.

### What If a Customer Asks for an ATS Upgrade?

BPP's scope is the inlet box and interlock kit installation — this is the "Storm-Ready Connection System." For customers specifically wanting a fully automatic system, the honest answer is: "That's a different scope and a bigger investment — we can talk about what that looks like, but for most portable generator owners, the interlock approach gives you everything you need at a fraction of the cost."

## TOPIC 7: SURGE PROTECTOR INFORMATION

### What Is a Whole-Home Surge Protector in This Context?

A **whole-home surge protector** (also called a Service Protection Device / SPD) is a device installed at or in the main electrical panel that monitors incoming voltage and diverts any voltage spike above a threshold to ground — before that spike can reach the home's appliances, electronics, and wiring.

In the context of a generator installation, BPP offers this as an **add-on at the time of install for $375** (parts + labor, installed). The device is a permanent fixture in the panel — it is not a plug-in power strip.

### What Specific Events Does It Protect Against?

**Generator-related events:**
1. **Generator startup surge**: When the generator first starts, output voltage and frequency are not immediately stable — it can briefly produce voltage spikes before settling. The SPD absorbs this before it reaches appliances.
2. **Generator shutdown spike**: When the generator is turned off or runs out of fuel abruptly, the collapsing magnetic field in the generator windings can produce a brief voltage spike.
3. **Load shedding spikes**: When a large motor (well pump, AC compressor) switches off, the stored energy in the motor's inductance briefly creates a voltage spike on the circuit — this can propagate through the panel.
4. **Utility power restoration surge**: When utility power is restored after an outage, the sudden return of line power can sometimes arrive with a brief spike or sag before stabilizing. The SPD absorbs the spike.

**Non-generator events (ongoing protection):**
5. **Lightning-induced surges**: A lightning strike near the home or on utility lines can drive a massive transient spike through the service entrance. A whole-home SPD is the first line of defense (though a direct strike can overwhelm any SPD — CO detectors and sensitive electronics on individual surge strips add a second layer).
6. **Utility grid switching surges**: Utility companies switching capacitor banks, transformer taps, or other grid equipment creates transient spikes that enter through the service entrance.

### NEC Code Note

Under the 2020 NEC (which SC has adopted), Section 230.67 requires a **Surge Protective Device to be installed at the service when a service is installed or replaced**. An interlock installation modifies the service panel, which means an SPD is technically required under a strict reading of the 2020 NEC. Installing it at the time of the generator install is the right time to do it.

### Installation

The SPD mounts inside or adjacent to the main electrical panel:
- **Type 2 SPD** (most common for residential): Snaps into the panel like a breaker, occupying one to two breaker slots, with a connection to the main lugs or breaker bus. This is the standard installation for BPP.
- **Type 1 SPD** (service entrance): Installed on the line side of the main disconnect; more comprehensive but more complex installation. Typically a separate add-on job.

Installation is a 15–30 minute addition to the generator install day — Key does it at the same time, which is why bundling it makes sense.

### Brands BPP Works With

Common and reputable whole-home SPD brands used in residential panel installations:
- **Eaton** (CHSPT2ULTRA or similar — connects to CH/BR series panels that are common in Upstate SC)
- **Siemens** (FS140 FirstSurge — connects to Siemens/Murray panels)
- **Leviton** (Panel Protector series)
- **Intermatic** (IG1240RC or similar)
- **Schneider Electric / Square D** (HEPD80 or SDSA series for Square D panels)

Key selects the SPD appropriate for the customer's panel brand.

### When Is the $375 Surge Protector Recommended?

**Always** — but especially:
- Homes with a lot of electronics (smart TVs, home office computers, gaming systems, smart home devices)
- Homes in areas with frequent thunderstorms (all of Upstate SC qualifies — we get significant lightning activity in summer)
- Homes with any medical equipment (CPAP, oxygen concentrator, home dialysis equipment) — equipment failure from a surge can be life-threatening
- Homes where the generator will be used regularly (more transitions = more surge exposure)
- Newer homes with variable-speed HVAC, smart appliances, and inverter-driven devices that are particularly sensitive to voltage transients

### The Sales Case for the Surge Protector

The generator connection creates two new power transition events every time the customer uses it: startup and shutdown. Each event is a potential surge. The $375 add-on is positioned as: "You're about to create a system that transitions power twice every outage. The surge protector is what makes sure that transition doesn't damage the appliances you were trying to protect in the first place."

**Frame it as protection of the investment, not an upsell.** The customer just spent $1,400–$2,000 to protect their home during outages. The surge protector protects that protection system and everything plugged into it.
